Casper H. Blaauw

Studying MSc Bioinformatics @ UCPH · Bioinformatics Student Assistant @ Clinical Microbiomics · Intern @ Winther group & Joshi group

I'm a bioinformatician, currently studying the MSc Bioinformatics at the University of Copenhagen and working as a part-time bioinformatician at Clinical Microbiomics. Besides this, I am a research intern working with Hiren Joshi and Ole Winther and I'm the president of CBioVikings, the Danish regional student group of the ISCB.

My main interest is understanding how systems come together to form a greater whole. These can range from subway systems to online communities, but my primary focus is understanding the rules behind the molecular mechanisms of life, whether you call it -omics, ML, systems biology, or something else. I went into Molecular Life Sciences at Utrecht University hoping to study this in the lab, but realised my questions were best answered behind a computer instead. The rest of my four years there were dedicated to biology, programming and statistics (including a minor in Applied Data Science), with a detour to the University of Hong Kong to study public policy and international relations. Alongside this, I worked in a variety of roles in education (from tutor to substitute teacher) for those four years.

Besides bioinformatics, I care about properly understanding and visualising data, science and technology's impact on society, and (inter)national public policy. In my free time, some of my passions are baking, cities, design, languages, and obscure geographical knowledge. To learn more about me, see my CV on LinkedIn, explore my code on GitHub, or scroll down for more details about my research and motivation.

Language modeling of proteins for glycosylation site prediction

University of Copenhagen

Used BERT-based language models, trained on UniProt rather than Wikipedia, to distill protein sequences to their core grammatical context in the language of life. Armed with the contextual representations for a variety of glycosylated sequences, I set out to figure out the rules behind O-GalNAc glycosylation, a biological process under complex multi-level regulation.

About me

[Under construction] I always like to say that I ended up in life sciences due to a single gif, which solidified my fascination for the molecular mechanisms of life. However, it had fierce competition from programmes like computer science, environmental science, human geography, and economics & policy when picking my bachelor's degree (which you do before enrolling in the Netherlands) and the systems and society approaches from those fields are something that I still try to apply within bioinformatics. I did really like biology and chemistry's experiment-based approach to science though, and so I enrolled in Molecular Life Sciences. The programme, primarily hosted by the chemistry department, did a great job in emphasising understanding over memorisation and research over rote learning. This mindset really shaped me as a budding scientist, even if I left the chemistry department behind after my first year. Their research and teaching, although excellent, was focused on structural biology, and my dreams were bigger: understanding genomes was much more of my thing than painstakingly figuring out individual protein structures. Instead, I indulged in programming and statistics, as deciphering (biological) datasets to understand large-scale biology was what really got my attention. In a way, that was a natural fit, as it led me back to the choice between computer science and molecular life science I had to make years prior, and even included the systems view I liked regardless of field.

At the end of my Molecular Life Sciences bachelor, it was very clear I wanted to go fully into bioinformatics and leave the wetlab behind, especially as I'd been doing a minor in Applied Data Science and a computational thesis. Looking around Europe, I decided to go study in Copenhagen: perhaps the biggest life sciences hub in Europe, with great bioinformatics faculty hosting a great MSc programme, in a lovely city to boot. Here, I have the opportunity to get acquainted with leading bioinformatics work in both academia and industry.
